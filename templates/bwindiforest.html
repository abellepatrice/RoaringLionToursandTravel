Bwindi Impenetrable Forest

This can easily be described as Uganda’s most popular park as a Gorilla Trekking base. It is referred to as Impenetrable due to its thick forestation that makes accessibility of the park’s interior difficult. It was declared a UNESCO Natural World Heritage site in 1994 protecting about half of the world’s mountain Gorilla population. It is however quite small in size covering about 321 km². Bwindi boasts 17 habituated Gorilla Families split in four bases around the park. Viewing is limited to 8 people per family and for a maximum of 1 hour.


Activities in Bwindi Impenetrable National Park

Gorilla Trekking: Embark on a once-in-a-lifetime adventure to track and observe mountain gorillas in their natural habitat.
Nature Walks: Explore the dense forests of Bwindi on guided nature walks, encountering diverse flora and fauna along the way.
Birdwatching: With over 350 bird species, including Albertine Rift endemics, Bwindi is a paradise for bird enthusiasts.
Cultural Visits: Engage with local communities, such as the Batwa Pygmies, and learn about their traditional way of life and cultural practices.

Wildlife in Bwindi Impenetrable National Park

Mountain Gorillas: Encounter the gentle giants of Bwindi as you trek through the forest and observe these fascinating primates up close.
Other Primates: In addition to gorillas, Bwindi is home to other primate species such as chimpanzees, monkeys, and baboons.
Small Mammals: Spot a variety of small mammals including forest elephants, duikers, and bushbucks during your explorations.
Rich Biodiversity: Discover the incredible diversity of plant and insect life that thrives within the confines of the impenetrable forest.

Best Time to Visit

Dry Season (June to September): The dry season offers ideal trekking conditions with less rainfall and clearer trails.
Wet Season (March to May and October to November): Despite increased rainfall, the wet season offers lush vegetation and fewer tourists.
Shoulder Seasons (December to February and June to September): These transitional periods provide a balance between weather conditions and visitor numbers.
